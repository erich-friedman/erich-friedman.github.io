Problem of the Month
(July 2013)

In August 2000, we investigated Friedman numbers. In April 2012, we investigated non-integral Friedman numbers, and almost Friedman numbers, both of which have been moved here for organizational purposes. This month we investigate two new related problems.

A fractional Friedman number is a terminating or repeating decimal that can be written in another way using its digits, the four basic arithmetic operations, exponentiation, and parentheses. Notice that a decimal point is required in the number, but not allowed in the expression to make that number. If a number can be written with its digits in order, we call it a nice fractional Friedman number and show it in red. What (nice) fractional Friedman numbers can you find?

A redundant Friedman number can be written with a subset of its digits. Given a set of digits that is not used, what is the smallest redundant Friedman number including those digits that can be written without those digits?
